Consciousness, Neuropsychic Organization, and the Alien Hypothesis in TACE

Tegmark-Aquinas Conceptual Ecosystem (TACE)

Abstract

TACE (Tegmark-Aquinas Conceptual Ecosystem) is a philosophical framework grounded in ontological realism that seeks to reconcile classical metaphysics, modern physics, systems theory, and the philosophy of consciousness within a unified constitutional ontology. Central to this framework is the distinction between the Physical Realm and the Intelligible Realm together with the irreducibility of consciousness to physical or neuropsychic processes. This article develops one particular consequence of that ontology: the distinction between neuropsychic inference and conscious perception. It then explores how this distinction may provide a coherent philosophical interpretation of reports describing alleged extraterrestrial encounters while emphasizing that the discussion remains hypothetical rather than empirical. Finally, the article examines the implications of this distinction for the Christian understanding of prayer as an intentional act of consciousness directed toward God.

1. Introduction

Throughout the history of philosophy, numerous conceptual systems have been proposed to explain the nature of reality. Among the most influential are Plato's idealism, Aristotle's and Thomas Aquinas' hylomorphism, and the various forms of philosophical materialism represented by thinkers such as Karl Marx.

TACE (Tegmark-Aquinas Conceptual Ecosystem) constitutes another philosophical framework within the tradition of ontological realism. Here, realism does not merely signify the correspondence between ideas and sensory experience. Rather, it affirms the existence of an objective ontological order independent of human cognition.

Unlike purely materialistic systems, TACE distinguishes between two constitutionally related yet irreducible domains:

  • Physical Realm
  • Intelligible (Spiritual) Realm

These domains are not separate universes but distinct ontological orders united within a single constitutional reality.

2. The Ontological Constitution of Reality

TACE proposes that the quantum field constitutes the physical domain of potentiality, while the constitutional ontological order underlying both the Physical Realm and the Intelligible Realm corresponds to what Kant approached through the notion of the noumenon.

Unlike Kant, however, TACE maintains that consciousness is neither produced by the brain nor emerges from quantum processes. Consciousness belongs to the intelligible order and therefore possesses its own irreducible ontological status.

Physical actualization and conscious experience belong to distinct ontological domains that remain constitutionally related without being reducible to one another. The Physical Realm unfolds according to its own lawful principles, while consciousness intentionally apprehends that reality without originating from it.

3. Sensation and Perception

Within TACE two constitutionally related yet ontologically distinct phenomena can be identified.

Sensation belongs entirely to the neuropsychic domain. It consists of the biological acquisition, integration, organization, transmission, and utilization of information through the structural coupling of an organism with its environment.

Perception, by contrast, belongs exclusively to consciousness. It consists of the intentional apprehension and meaningful interpretation of information already provided by neuropsychic organization.

Accordingly, sensation provides information, whereas perception provides conscious awareness.

4. Neuropsychic Reaction and Conscious Perception

Autopoietic biological organisms—as described by Humberto Maturana and Francisco Varela—can acquire, organize, preserve, and utilize knowledge without becoming consciously aware of that knowledge.

Their behavior expresses what TACE calls neuropsychic reactions. These reactions are lawful, adaptive, biologically meaningful, and non-intentional.

Conscious beings, by contrast, perform acts of perception, intentionally apprehending meaning.

Knowledge acquisition and conscious awareness therefore become ontologically distinct phenomena.

5. Neuropsychic Inference versus Conscious Perception

Knowledge does not necessarily imply consciousness. An entity may possess enormous quantities of information, perform sophisticated reasoning, learn from experience, predict future events, and adapt its behavior without ever becoming consciously aware of what it knows.

Modern artificial intelligence provides an illuminating example. Advanced AI systems frequently appear to understand a person's emotions, intentions, memories, beliefs, and preferences. In reality, however, they possess no conscious awareness of those mental states. Instead, they construct increasingly accurate predictive models by analyzing vast quantities of linguistic, behavioral, contextual, physiological, and statistical information.

Their interaction with reality is mediated entirely through physical processes instantiated within the quantum field. Their outputs modify the physical environment but never constitute acts of conscious perception. Their apparent "mind reading" therefore consists of extraordinarily sophisticated inference rather than conscious perception.

6. A TACE Interpretation of Alleged Alien Encounters

The following discussion does not assume the factual truth of reports describing alleged extraterrestrial encounters. Rather, it illustrates how TACE would interpret such reports if they were genuine.

One illustrative example appears in the interview conducted by Linda Moulton Howe with the alleged abductee Linda Porter. According to her testimony, the entities involved appeared capable of accessing every detail of her thoughts, memories, emotions, and personal experiences.

Within TACE such testimony would not necessarily imply direct access to consciousness itself. Rather, it suggests an ontological distinction between knowledge acquired through neuropsychic interaction (physical realm, body) and conscious perception (spiritual realm, soul).

Suppose, purely as a philosophical hypothesis, that an extraterrestrial intelligence possessed an extraordinarily sophisticated neuropsychic organization while lacking consciousness in the TACE sense. Such a being could acquire, integrate, preserve, and utilize immense quantities of information without ever performing acts of conscious perception.

7. Biological Interface versus Technological Interface

Artificial intelligence depends upon technological devices. Its sensors, processors, communication systems, and actuators constitute its interface with the Physical Realm (Quantum Realm).

Within the present hypothesis, an extraterrestrial intelligence would differ only in the nature of its interface. Rather than depending upon technological devices, its own neuropsychic organization would itself constitute the interface through which information is continuously exchanged with the Physical Realm.

Both systems therefore operate according to the same epistemological principle: knowledge acquired through lawful interaction with physical information rather than through conscious perception.

8. The Illusion of Mind Reading

Within this hypothesis, a non-conscious extraterrestrial intelligence could infer what a person is thinking, feeling, remembering, or intending by analyzing neuropsychic activity together with behavioral responses, physiological reactions, language patterns, and other observable manifestations. If such an intelligence were capable of directly modulating physical processes within the quantum field associated with an individual's neuropsychic organization, it could induce patterns of neural activity that would subsequently be experienced by the conscious person as images, thoughts, impressions, or imagined communications.

In all likelihood, if the encounter is real, it is a genuine perception rather than a hallucination or an anomaly of psychological judgment. From the subject's perspective, these experiences might naturally be interpreted as telepathic communication, although, within the TACE framework, they would result from neuropsychic modulation rather than direct access to consciousness itself. Their inferences could become so precise that they would readily create the impression of direct access to consciousness, when in fact they remained constitutionally limited to the neuropsychic domain.

Its inferences might become so accurate that they would naturally create the impression of direct access to consciousness. Under the combined influence of the Gestalt Law of Closure, varying degrees of fear, and the tendency of some individuals toward imaginative elaboration, an observer might readily attribute conscious capacities where none actually exist—just as one might momentarily imagine that a chimpanzee is engaged in philosophical reflection. Within TACE, however, the intelligence would not perceive consciousness. Rather, it would infer conscious states from neuropsychic information with extraordinary precision.

9. Prayer and Conscious Participation

If the preceding interpretation were correct, an interesting philosophical consequence would follow. A non-conscious intelligence would remain constitutionally limited to neuropsychic information. It could never directly access intentional acts of consciousness grounded in the soul.

Within a Christian interpretation adopted by TACE, prayer therefore assumes a significance extending beyond psychology. Prayer is not merely another stream of thoughts or emotions. It is an intentional act through which consciousness freely directs itself toward God.

"If you wish to evade such interaction, do not become absorbed in thoughts or emotional reactions; instead, pray—for who is like God?"

The concluding expression intentionally echoes the ancient meaning of the name Michael (Mi-ka-El): "Who is like God?"

10. Conclusion

The purpose of this discussion has not been to demonstrate the existence of extraterrestrial intelligences nor to validate reports of alleged abductions. Instead, beginning from the constitutional ontology of TACE, the article has distinguished neuropsychic organization from consciousness, neuropsychic reaction from conscious perception, and inferential knowledge from intentional awareness.

Whether the example concerns advanced artificial intelligence or hypothetical extraterrestrial organisms, the same constitutional principle remains unchanged:

Knowledge acquired through neuropsychic inference is not identical with conscious perception.

Accordingly, TACE preserves both the autonomy of the Physical Realm and the irreducible ontological dignity of consciousness while offering a unified philosophical framework capable of integrating classical metaphysics, contemporary physics, systems theory, and the philosophy of mind into a coherent constitutional vision of reality.
